    Notes about a discussion between Tanya, Sadun and myself about data sharing, documantation, and reconciliating different needs and perspectives.
    Published

    March 26, 2025

    These notes are from the CITF weekly update meeting and a brief discussion that followed. In the meeting, David asked Sadun to present a top-level account of the database and any complications or challenges that he has to deal with. According to David, they’ve been so focused on the details for so long, and he thinks it’s necessary to take a step back and review where things are at on a broader scale. This will be at one of our weekly updates, but David also wants to get together with Sadun and Annabelle to initiate a deeper dialogue between them. I never met Annabelle, but David and Tanya referred to her as someone who is pulling and analyzing data from across the databank, and who will have a distinct perspective from the rest of us.` In some ways, her role is similar to how I imagine Tina at Maelstrom. I requested to be present as an observer, and David seemed ok with that.

    David specifically referred to a need to account for two perspectives:

    • Us as users who are really close to the work, and who also administer the work
    • People we give data to, looking through their eyes, trying to understand how useful the data we give them is, and how much “hand-holding” is required

    There was also some reference to “extracts”, which I tentatively understand to mean the components that are very tightly integrated which may be put to analytical use.

    David mentioned that the incoming UNC data access request could be an opportunity to better understand users’ needs. I didn’t say it out loud in this meeting (maybe during tomorrow’s biweekly update with David) but I would love to sit in on those conversations too.

    After David ended the call, and after briefly chatting with Aklil about our focus group project, I joined a really fascinating discussion with Tanya and Sadun. It stemmed from topics we discussed during the meeting, namely about missing data and how to document these things for people who want to reuse them. But it also included discussion about some challenges of working with Maelstrom, with people who use the data, with the people who shared data from the original studies. The conversation had a very casual tone, and I wish I was able to record it. Midway through I told them that I wanted to take notes on the discussion from my memory of it, and I lamented about not obtaining informed consent. Tanya indicated her hesitancy on the basis of not especting this to be recorded or used in any way, so I offered to share my initial draft notes for them to review before incorporating into my dataset.

    These notes should not be treated as verbatim records. I tried to remember as much as I could, and despite my best efforts to be as descriptive as possible, notes may appear out of order and include some personal thoughts and interjections. I also removed some of my own thoughts when I sent the draft for Tanya and Sadun to review since I did not want them to be influenced by my own thoughts that occurred after the conversation ended.

    I entered the conversation as Tanya and Sadun were talking about how to handle the missing values. I intervened because I’m genuinely interested in these things and I thought I could lend some support, but also because I saw an opportunuty to gain some insight with regards to my postdoc research.

    They referred to the harmonized dataset as if it were one thing, and when I asked about this Tanya clarified what she meant by this. We used the example of harmonizing variables relating to sex, gender, sex assigned at birth, etc. I will fill in the details later, and in some ways that was less relevant to the overall scope of the conversation. But one aspect of this moment that I did find really interesting (and which was repeated at some point later on) is Tanya saying that their work is more like relating recorded variables than consolidating study design criteria. I think there may be more to unpack here, and somehow it aligns with the prospective / retrospective dichotomy raised by Isabel. Something to latch on to later.

    This transitioned to addressing how missing data are documented and how the harmonized overlaps are reported. Tanya showed us the broad table of harmonized variables, which indicate percentages of harmonized variables by study and percentages of harmonized studies by variable. Tanya expressed some concern that those percentages misrepresent how much was actually harmonized, and she cited an example of transport across provinces, but I was really unfamiliar with it and it was a really brief mention. It was a notable example because it was a rare (?) example of _under_estimation of harmonization potential, whereas in most conversations I’ve had about this kind of thing the topic has largely focused on flagging _over_confidence in datasets’ combined analytical potential. I asked about what they want to do to resolve this, and Tanya’s response was to maybe put a notice on the page (she didn’t use the term, but in my mind I was thinking “disclaimer”), which would draw attention to the tendency for this table to simplify the analytic potential. This was met with the caveat that they do not want to discourage reuse of the data by those who may not want to consult, or by reducing confidence in the data. She added that she wants to encourage users to get in touch so that Tanya, Sadun and others who are more familiar with the data could consult with users regarding the nuanced reality of the data’s completeness (as I’m writing this, I realize that Tanya was frequently using the term “missingness”, which is an interesting inversion of the more common “completeness”). I then suggested that they could break down the rows (representing recorded variables) into topical or thematic groupings, and determine percentages for each of those, and they were both receptive to that idea.

    When I commented on how this could be a quick fix, Tanya pointed out that while the CITF hosts the table, Maelstrom is really responsible for updating it. The conversation then shifted to their relationship with Maelstrom. Tanya said something really poignant, and I tried to capture it as accurately as possible but was unable to keep track at the end:

    Asking Maelstrom to do more makes us less agile. They take a very conservative approach, [whereas us as epidemiolgist come at it with more practical mindset that may require greater flexibility].

    At a certian point, not sure if it was at this moment or later, Tanya said something like “they [Maelstrom] have to took at feasibility, make recommendations, do a few cycles of review of the protocol, etc”, which implies its more of a bureaucratic process, a series of checks and balances, a series of procedures. Whereas David just wants to do the work, make it go. I’m also recalling another reference to their consultations with Maelstrom (I forget if it was before of after this point in time) that when they meet with Maelstrom, they always manage to make a strong and convincing case to be more conservative, to be more considered in their approach. Something in the way Tanya described it made it seem like an illusion, like they entered this new environment (the meeting with Maelstrom staff) and had a spell cast on them, or were reminded of the “proper” way to do things. And this really contrasts with our weekly meetings, where David encourages us to just go, produce something, talk things through casually, make it work in practical reality.

    Jumping forward and skipping in time to a later part of the discussion that is particularly relevant here. Tanya told us that she was never involved in data collection. She had used others’ data, and admitted (it felt a bit like an admission, even if it was just a guilt-neutral recollection) that she didn’t really think about how the data were being collected. She mentioned how physicians or those collecting data in clinical contexts would write down what they saw as relevant in the moment, and didn’t necessarily think about (or have to think about) what was to be done with the data. I related my experience with DObsiSS and how I had to put blinders on to simply get analytical results out of the project. Skipping even further ahead, to when we were standing to leave the meeting, Tanya said in the most eloquent way (I really wish I was recording everything, but especially this bit) about how people in labs (she referenced lab people specifically) are filling in their spreadsheets, with little concern for how the data would be used. And this wasn’t said with antagonism, it was actually with great empathy, and perhaps with a renewed understanding of the challenges that she learned while working with CITF in relation to her work doing “pure” data analysis for her doctoral research, and perhaps with a bit of envy for their blissful ignorance.

    This all relates to the idea that data workers, who are explicitly data workers in identity, tooling, background, community of practice, etc, and especially those who work with an eye toward developing research infrastructure, have a more finely fined curatorial lens, since they must constantly think about reconciling differences between inputs and outcomes. This is also true on a “grander scale” with Maelstrom, who work on a more abstract level, and who cope with these challenges through adherence to procedure. And I’m sure that from the perspective of the study originators, CITF is very procedural too, perhaps even to a fault, in their minds. Which again brings me back to the notion that data management is management, and how data sharing infrastructures mask low-scale collaborative experiences using technical and managerial processes and logics.

    I chimed in saying that this whole thing is the reason why I’m here. Sadun then interjected with a poignant statement, which actually reflects something I was thinking about on the bus ride to work today:1 that none of this is good or bad, it just is. We just have to muddle through it. Our actions are not good or bad either, but they do affect how people will approach the data. In other words, our actions as infrastructure maintainers are commitments to certain norms, expectations, and activity-pathways.

    Turning back to an earlier part of the discussion, we were talking about the people who share their data. Sadun mentioned how the research assistants may change and communicating with them could be really hard because they weren’t there when the data were originally collected and are therefore unable to communicate the nuanced aspects of the data that Sadun needs to document. At one point Tanya also mentioned that Sadun’s work fundamentally entails accounting for all the info shared during emails or zoom calls with study originators. My sense is that this is happening in terms of drawing relations among variables, and adding notes in a column of a spreadsheet about these decisions, but these are not really documented in a formal or digestible way.

    So the task that they have in mind now is to more comprehensively document the data so that users can figure out what is available and how to make effective use of them. I raised the example of API documentation, which not only define the scope of each variable but include instructions for how to access the data, which bear hints of what they may be used for in example code and vignettes.

    I asked why they can’t just give all the data to the users, and I guess this was a bit of a silly question because they both immediately thought that was a bad idea. I asked why the users can’t just figure out how to use the data on their own, and they said that they want to ensure that users know how to use the data, navigate through it all, and use it in a way that actually reflects the data’s capabilities and limitations.2

    I joked that we should simply put the users in touch with the originators and be done with it, and Tanya remarked that by inviting users to consult with us to gain a better understanding of the dataset they could also maybe loop in the studies’ originators. I also remember that in this remark, Tanya used the term “service” to refer to this consultation, which in my mind (thinking about the logic of management) framed this as something that can be made into a standardized procedure.

    At some point Tanya mentioned how there are sill so many studies left to ingest, and we started talking about how that works. Many studies have signed data sharing agreements, and among them, some have given some data and many have not yet provided any at all. Tanya said that some studies are adamant about getting as many publications as they can before they share the data. I responded by asking about whether this means the data were properly processed because of the work that they had put in prior to analysis, and she shrugged, not wanting to generalize.

    Then she went on to talk about a few different kinds of situations in which data sharing might occur (the prior example being a situation in which data sharing does not occur). She said that some people are very into it, and will put a lot of effort to document their work. Others are more reluctant, or do it to clear the task off their list and move on. I suggested that maybe these latter participants would be less likely to respond to follow up emails, and without saying anything I recall Sadun gave a knowing grin in response to that remark. But then Tanya also recalled a specific situation where a PI was entirely non-responsive via email, but when they finally got around to meeting on a zoom call he was very thorough in his descriptions of the data and how they should be used. The point of this was to say that professors are just really busy and get so many emails all the time, so you can’t really read too much into it.

    Footnotes

    1. I was thinking about how to present my work in a way that makes it seem like I’m not judging them, in acticipation of my MCHI talk next week.↩︎

    2. This really reminded me of the dataARC workshop at the CAA2018 conference, which really inspired my research on archaeological data sharing. DataARC is a pan-disciplinary database on the human ecodynamics of iceland, and researchers were very concerned about how to effectively communicate the disciplinary knowledge necessary to make sense of data they contributed, so that they are not misused. What I concluded from this workshop session, in which they solicited feedback (and which no one was able to really provide) is that they really wanted a collaborative relationship, which was in tension with their desire to make their data openly accessible for anyone to use. I’m seeing the exact same tension here at the CITF.↩︎
