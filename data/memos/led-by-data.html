Being led by data

Some thoughts on how epidemiological research is led by data, and how authority is ascribed to data.
Published

April 4, 2025

Modified

July 8, 2026

Yesterday I had two interesting discussions that I think I should reflect on here.

The first was a bit more drawn out throughout the day with various PhD students who sit near me. It was largely in response to my MCHI presentation where I gave an overview of my research protocol and methods. We were dicussing the challenges of communicating qualitative research values to quantitative researchers, and the weird vision of research that comes along with quant approaches.

It went into concerns about causality, and the division between causal and non-causal research in epidemiology. According to one of the students, there are three main tenets of causal research:

  1. There has to be an intervention
  2. XXXX (I forget)
  3. The intervention should be the only difference

There is strong emphasis on causal research as being more valid or rigorous. And people who are doing non-causal research have to really make it clear that they are drawing associations rather than causal claims. Non-causal research is often referred to as descriptive research, whereas causal is often called explanatory. The causal research is therefore considered more scientific since it arrives as more generalizable and context-independent truths, i.e. the explanations.

However, the research that my student colleagues are doing is more descriptive, largely because there is no intervention involved. At the same time, it seems directly tied in to policy development, and highlights potential harms through the associations they highlight. And by being rooted in real-world data, rather than artificial laboratory-like conditions, it captures something that is closer to reality. And yet, their work is somehow de-valued with regards to the capacity to inform policy recommendations.

We also discussed how this department is very steeped in this belief in the primacy of causal research, and yet it is also very focused on surveillance, which is largely descriptive in nature. I speculated that this may be because of the sources of the data. Surveillance research such as the work that my student colleagues are doing mainly relies on data produced by government agencies. These data reflect all the healthcare provided to all people who used government health services. It therefore has a feeling of being more “natural”, in the sense that it’s not created with any specific intent.1 It may have gaps, based on political biases with regards to access to healthcare, but epi tends to be very apolitical and does not really question these gaps that much. In any case, I want to contrast this with data generated by researchers with intent, which carries their own biases that epidemiologists feel more comfortable accounting for and reflecting upon, since they are aspects of study design rather than political challenges.

I think this sense of naturalness, and the general sense that it’s ok to ignore or scope out the gaps of government data (which does not exist with data collected with intent), surveillance research is considered a more legitimate kind of descriptive or non-causal research. And in my mind it should work the other way around — the data collected with intent should be considered more legitimate since it is easier to account for the blind spots. But it acutally makes sense if you recognize ignorance as a major factor. More specifically, tacit and collective recognition of what is ok to ignore. Drawing attention to flaws implies that something better can be done instead of the way things are currently being done. It is much easier to critcize a colleague’s paper on the basis of a decision they made than it is to criticize a public dataset that no one really designed — or that ended up the way it is through massive social inequities. It’s an imbalance of deciding which data speak for themselves (which implies their immutability) and which ones are derived from human decisions and actions, which of course plays into the heightened value of objectivity and the lessened value of subjectivity.

Later on, at a social gathering of postdocs from across campus, I was talking with a new friend who works in engineering. I was ranting a bit about these things I just described, and about other frustrating interactions with and observations of epidemiologists. One of these perennial frustrations is the lack of common sense, or disconnect with reality, that is common among epidemiologists. For instance, I’ve witnessed epidemiologists struggle to prove statistically something that is just plainly evident to anyone who knows anything about the social circumstances involved. I’m thinking specifically about the challenges of modelling the spread of Covid-19 across Toronto — it was plainly evident that the reasons why the virus spread more in North York and Scarborough is because they are racialized communities comprised largely of service workers, whereas the more central neighbourhoods are more white and have greater ability to work from home. And yet, they felt an urgent need to explain this in a much more convoluted way.

I explained this to my new friend as being led by the data and the methods, which gets in the way of arriving at some stable truth. He contrasted this with his experience looking at materials’ conductivity. His experiments involve subjecting materials to an electric charge using some equipment that creates and channels the charge in a directed way (this should not be taken as an authoritative description at all.) He generates several data points, and most are clustered around a common tendence, but once in a while one reading may be way off. For him, it makes sense to simply disregard this reading, just throw it out, based on his expertise and experience working with this machine and knowing that it should not have done this. But for epidemiologists, they take this kind of abberation very seriously, and have to find a way to model it out. In other words, they don’t take their expertise seriously — they are led by the data.

I also remarked an observation from the MCHI seminars, which present students’ preliminary research findings. In some of these presentations, they note some abberations, and profs in the room sometimes politely try to assuage the students’ anxiety about these errors by drawing from a casual and anecdotal experience that others in the dataset may have also experienced, which could produce these blips. My sense is that this is done as a kindness for the students’ presenting in a semi-public setting. However, this is not really a legitimate way to account for these blips, and when a student then returns to their desk they can’t really incorporate that into their analysis, which produces a lot of anxiety. This represents a mismatched access to familiarity with the mechanisms of research — akin to the engineer who is intimately familiar with the equipment that produces a charge. I’ve noticed that students in my department, even doctoral students, are not being integrated into a community of practice, and this seems like the biggest crime to me, since this is where you learn about what blips are normal and can be dismissed with the wave of a hand. They don’t have confidence in their ability to lead the research, since the data are in control. It also does not really help that most profs who offer this kind of remark are white and the students are not white; students’ must account for anecdotal experiences that are not aligned with their own experiences, and perhaps more relevant to my own work (but which I have admittedly not really felt comfortable asking about yet), they sometimes defer to the authority of the data since their agency as researchers is curtailed by their racialized identities.
