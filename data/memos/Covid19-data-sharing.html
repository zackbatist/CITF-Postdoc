Covid-19 and data sharing

Overview of takes on data sharing with regards to Covid-19 research. Top 20 papers from google scholar.
Published

May 29, 2025

Modified

July 8, 2026

@cosgriff2020: A very brief, exasperated statement of disbelief in the fact that there are no systems in place to pool and share healthcare data. Cited Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care (MIMIC) as an example of how to properly share intensive care data, without going into details. Explicitly states that the system is valuable for clinicians and computer scientists to develop predictive models; why are these communities specified? Seems almost like an admonition of the academic research community, in favour of clinical perspectives.

Although they call for robust data-sharing initiatives, they do mention the success of word-of-mouth and anecdotal information sharing:

In the first few weeks of the pandemic, knowledge about the disease and its treatment was generated from sharing of anecdotal observations and small case series. Although health-care professionals use modern technology to communicate, never before has the failure to build robust data-sharing systems for large-scale near real-time analysis in health care been more obvious.

@rios2020: Pits open access against open data. Naive vision of what open data will afford. Assumes an “overall belief” in the value and virtue of open data, and depicts inaction as a series of practical and logistical barriers, or selfish scientists holding back progress.

@gao2020: A survey of Covid-19 data sharing in the US. Breaks down several kinds of data, or domains in which data are generated. More of a technical survey of tools and protocols that support data capture and sharing, includin laboratory information management systems (LIMS) and electronic health records (EHR) systems and networks. Identifies common UI elements on data sharing websites, which are pretty standard (search boxes, clean UI, ability to filter data, etc). Touch on some challenges: lack of informed consent, lack of consistency, use of additional third-party and often commercial data sources (i.e. maps data). Also touch on researchers’ ignorance of best ways to organize data, of copyright and licensing conditions, or where data are actually located, etc, as well as apprehensions relating to collaborative commitments

The most crucial elements in overcoming these data sharing challenges are collaboration and communication between the parties (individuals or enterprises) while being diplo matic about each party’s wants and needs and employing a liaison if necessary.

Proposes a large-scale and centralized system for sharing data within each domain, for each country in a data sharing network. Also refers to large-scale mobile phone tracking as a key factor in successfully managing the pandemic, particularly in China, Korea and Singapore.

@fegan2021: A response to @cosgriff2020, outlining the work being done by the COVID-19 Clinical Research Coalition. Highlights calls for data sharing by the Wellcome Trust and the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation, and draws attention to persistent challenges, specifically the challenges of obtaining broad consent, lack of data management capacity, and potential to exasperate inequities across research centres. Reports on their initiative to provide researchers with practical advice and tooling, including recommended language for obtaining broad consent and guidance for establishing data sharing agreements. I generally get the sense that these are the sober people in the room, aware of their surroundings and doing what they can to balance potential benefits with responsibilities to stakeholders at greater risk of harm.

@maxwell2003: A survey of data sharing initiatives that specifically focuses on their adherence to FAIR principles and practices. A qualitative evaluation, similar to the work I’m doing with Alex Bernier. There is a section looking at harmonization:, which found greater emphasis on prospective rather than retrospective harmonization. However I wonder whether this is biased by the sample, which seems more oriented by clinical data sources.

The findings accounting for tendencies for adherence to FAIR are also notable:

Platforms that met all of the criteria for FAIRness were large, government-funded platforms that pre-existed COVID-19. The five registries that met all four criteria for FAIRness collected clinical data only, harmonised this data prospectively, and had data access committees (DACs) in place to oversee the use of data. Registries that met none of the criteria for FAIRness were generally COVID-19- specific resources that are still accepting data and might develop in terms of infrastructure and governance for COVID-19 data collection and sharing in the future. The application of a community-developed standard for participant-level data or metadata was the most commonly missed component of FAIRness, only four (36%) of 11 platforms and eight (18%) of 44 registries that collected participant-level clinical data had adopted a community developed data capture or exchange standard.

Basically, doing open data for the sake of it is not successful. When more thought goes into it, when it’s based on informed and professional standards, it is more successful. In other words, it’s more work than it seems, and having some prior institutional knowledge goes a long way.

Also, very little community engagement, i.e. no return on investment for those who contributed data.

I need to return to this paper later. The discussion is very rich, and I am not currently in the headspace to read it closely.

@foraker2021:* Presents a series of “lessons learned” from failured to implement the CDC’s prior data sharing protocols that emerged from the Ebola and Zika outbreaks. Distinguishes between short-term and long-term approaches, and describes challenges pertaining to each. Short term challenges include:

  • Inadequate tech and data infrastructure
  • Duplication of data requests and inconsistent technical requirements
    • “To overcome these barriers, we engaged in a process of co-design with the various public health departments to identify shared, com mon data elements and actionable data transfer formats or transac tional standards, so as to simplify and systematize these requests.”
  • Insufficient coordination of efforts by responsible entities or authorities

They propose some recommendations for long-term improvement:

  • Identify and fill technology gaps
    • “Given the urgency of the need for this infrastructure, we focused initially on repurposing existing technologies and platforms to enable rapid data sharing and data col lection, followed by quick iterations and continuous improvements on the use of these technologies. As we progress, and move toward the ideal state as shown in Figure 2, we would prefer to switch to existing or updated technologies that may be superior yet may take longer to implement.”
  • Pursue collaborative design of data sharing requirements and transmission mechanisms
    • “Data sharing efforts can involve transactional electronic data exchange, manual abstraction and reporting, platform data aggrega tion, and extract, transform, and load functions that are often auto mated processes to populate shared repositories or data marts. Of note, as data transmission activities mature over the course of this pandemic, processes can evolve from manual abstraction to that of automated queries and transmission via secure file transfer protocols, HL7 feeds, or fast health care interoperability resources approaches. The mechanism of data transmission must adapt as standards-based data workflows become more routine.”
    • “The unglamorous work of assessing and adjudicating data elements must be conducted before the data are entered into a data mart or other organized data management system. As these data are originating from fragmented systems, data cleaning and quality assurance activities will be invalu able to detect inconsistent or incomplete data before these data are relied upon for insights. Data definitions, even that of patients with a COVID-19 positive test, can vary widely across data sources and dis crepancies must be addressed early. Data feeds from collaborating entities must be transformed into analyzable datasets to meet the needs at local, regional, and national levels as shown in Figure 1.”
  • Facilitate cross-domain discussions involving legal and research compliance
    • “Early in the pandemic, data elements were needed to determine the availability of COVID-19 testing, the number of patients currently in the hospital and intensive care units, supply chain needs, and distribution of cases by geography. As our region reached its peak, our efforts partially shifted to simulate the post-COVID-19 era, determining the potential impacts of relaxing social distancing restrictions and predicting the next hotspot or cluster of new cases.”
  • Establish or participate in multi-institutional convening or coordinating activities
    • Create and sustain datasharing communities of practice comprised of organizations with complementary needs, expertise, and capabilities.
    • Share best practices and resources across entities.

This is a very important article. It is notable that it emphasizes collaborative, practical and administrative factors, and the importance of fostering long-term planning before the pandemic hits, establishing a culture of cooperation to counter the chaos that ensued early during the pandemic.

One notable aspect is its emphasis on providing longer term technology support. The recognition that epidemiologists were pulling together scrappy research infrastructure is reflexted in the case of CITF, but I wonder if this is something that can really be avoided. I also wonder if the building up of a tech stack is part of the legitimizing process, setting down stakes to build a village and claim the land — very public, very dramatic, how roots are settled and people make a nest for themselves. Removing the dramatic and performative aspect of this may not be desirable.

@tacconelli2022: Overview of challenges experienced in European Covid-19 data sharing initiatives. Relates to prior work from the Zika and Ebola outbreaks. Specific challenges they emphasize are lack of consensus on what standards to implement and lack of data science literacy in the health sector.

They call out sime limitations of harmonization, specfically the lack of documentation about decisions, which is something that my research directly responds to:

Initiatives such as Maelstrom Research have provided data har monisation guidelines that ensure data quality, reproducibility and transparency of the process Unfortunately, the process of harmonising and integrating data from existing projects is often poorly documented. Human resources and economic efforts should be made available to support the development and implementation of international standards and protocols for harmonisation.

They go over the objectives and operational contexts for a few European initiatives, namely ReCoDID, ORCHESTRA, unCoVer and SYNCHROS.

They then present centralized and federated approaches as distinct approaches that may be taken, presenting benefits and challenges for each. They do not mention the challenges that Julie Bergeron experienced with regards to DataShield — it’s a farily superficial overview.

They then go on to briefly propose some changes to the way funding calls are made. They propose adding requirements to funding calls to facilitate harmonization and integration, namely required language and consent conditions in informed consent documents, and requirements for adhering to data standards. Seems to draw from open science mandates in funding calls, particularly from the Gates Foundation and Wellcome Trust.

Seems like a simple call for people to make it easier to share a reuse data. Doesn’t rock the boat at all, but is begging for more food to feed the data science beast (their earlier emphasis on data science literacy is quite telling).

Their halfhearted reflection on centralized versus decentralized approaches is also very superficial, refusing to acknowledge key practical challenges. It’s also notable how they basically negate their whole argument for the benefits of federated analysis with a tepid statement acknowledging that centralized approaches may be better.

@tan2022: Presenting a blockchain-based approach. Given how popular bitcoin and blockchain was during the pandemic, it is not surprising that this came up as a highly cited paper. Not gonna give it much more attention.

@lucas-dominguez2021: Analysis of gold and green open access publishing of papers relating to Covid-19. Published in a scientometrics journal by historians of science. A fairly standard quantitative scientometric approach, tracking change over time (for the first five months of the pandemic), a network of international collaborations, and analysis of the kinds of supplementary materials associated with papers.

The analysis of supplementary material showed that the most frequent types of file were mainly PDF and DOC, which were found in 42.75% and 32.56% of the articles, respectively (Table 2). These files were mostly figures and tables related to DNA, RNA, protein sequence analysis and molecular signalling pathways; other frequently observed documents are checklists, clinical protocols and informed consents.

@watsom2022: A “news feature” scaremongering over fraudulent preprints. Conflates pre-review and post-review preprints. There is a section about flaws in the peer review model, and a sense of nihilism in the who academic enterprise. Also a section on unreliable meta-analyses, using one example of a meta-analysis on ivermectin. Safe to ignore this paper in the future, or to use it as a counter-example.

@dron2021:

  • The ORCHESTRA cohort is a similar initiative in Europe, which has also engaged in data harmonization activities [@rinaldi2022].
  • CORD-19: A resource of scientific papers on Covid-19 and related historical coronavirus research. CORD-19 is designed to facilitate the development of text mining and information retrieval systems over its rich collection of metadata and structured full text papers [@wang2020].
  • COVID-19 Canada Open Data Working Group.
  • See also @lin2022, @cheng2024 and @cameron2025 for overviews of some challenges involved in harmonizing Covid-19 data, without any direct reference to CITF of ORCHESTRA.
